Image Arithmetic Multiplication

Multiply two images pixel-by-pixel. This operation is commonly used for masking, blending, and texture modulation.

How Multiplication Works

Image multiplication combines two images by multiplying corresponding pixel values channel-by-channel. To keep values in the 0–255 range, the product is scaled by 255.

The Formula

Result(x,y) = (ImageA(x,y) × ImageB(x,y)) ÷ 255

• Operates on R, G, B independently

• Results are clamped to the 0–255 range

• Values near 0 darken; 255 preserves the other image

Typical Uses

Masking: Multiply by a mask to keep or attenuate regions.

Lighting/Textures: Modulate textures with light maps.

Blending: Darken-over blend where black removes and white keeps.

Mathematical Insights

Properties of Multiplication

Commutative: A × B = B × A

Associative: (A × B) × C = A × (B × C)

Identity: A × 255 = A (white image leaves A unchanged)

Annihilator: A × 0 = 0 (black image removes content)

Common Pitfalls

Gamma / Color Space: Multiply in linear space; sRGB multiplication darkens incorrectly.

Dynamic Range: Products compress mid-tones; consider normalization or exposure.

Zeros in Masks: Exact zeros permanently remove detail; soften masks to avoid hard cutouts.

Premultiplied Alpha: Don’t confuse color×alpha with alpha blending formulas.
